## Part 1: Strategic Mental Models

These are thinking frameworks for making better strategic decisions in marketing. They are not about persuading customers — they are about reasoning more clearly as a marketer.

---

### 1.1 First Principles Thinking

**What it is:** Breaking a problem down to its most fundamental truths (the things you know are definitely true) and reasoning up from there, rather than reasoning by analogy (what has worked before, what competitors do, what the industry convention is).

**The question it answers:** "Why do we assume this is the right approach?"

**How it works:** List all the assumptions embedded in your current approach. Challenge each one. Ask: "What do I know for certain about this situation?" Build your strategy from those certainties rather than from inherited convention.

**Marketing application:**

Example: A SaaS company assumes they need a free trial because "all SaaS companies offer free trials."

First principles challenge:
- Why do customers need a free trial? (To reduce perceived risk before purchasing.)
- Are there other ways to reduce perceived risk? (Money-back guarantee, case studies, demo, freemium, pilot program.)
- What specifically are customers uncertain about that makes the trial necessary? (Whether the product will actually work for their use case.)
- Could a 15-minute hands-on demo with a product specialist answer that question more effectively than a self-serve trial? (Possibly — and it also creates a sales conversation.)

The first principles approach does not conclude that free trials are bad. It concludes that the goal is risk reduction, and there may be better tools for it.

**When to use it:**
- When a marketing strategy is failing and you cannot identify why (your assumptions are the suspect).
- When entering a new market or category (competitor norms may not apply).
- When you want to genuinely differentiate rather than iterate on existing approaches.
- When a senior stakeholder says "but this is how it's always done" — that is a signal to apply first principles.

**Common examples in marketing:**
- "Why do we assume customers compare us against competitors? What if they are comparing us against doing nothing?"
- "Why do we assume longer sales cycles require more touchpoints? What if they require higher-quality touchpoints?"
- "Why do we send a weekly newsletter? What would we send if we had no precedent to follow?"

### 1.2 Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

**What it is:** People do not buy products; they hire products to do a job for them. That job has three dimensions: a functional job (the practical task), an emotional job (how they want to feel), and a social job (how they want to be perceived by others). The product that wins is the one that best does the full job the customer is trying to accomplish.

**Origin:** Developed by Clayton Christensen and popularized by Bob Moesta and Chris Spiek.

**The hiring metaphor:** When a customer buys your product, they are "firing" whatever they were using before (including doing nothing) and "hiring" your product to do a job. Understanding what job they are hiring it for — not what the product does — is the key to messaging.

**The three dimensions of a job:**

| Dimension | Question | Example (project management SaaS) |
|---|---|---|
| Functional | What does it help me do? | Stop missing deadlines and losing tasks between tools |
| Emotional | How does it make me feel? | Feel in control and confident rather than overwhelmed |
| Social | How does it make me look? | Appear organized and professional to my team and clients |

**Marketing application:**

Most product marketing focuses on functional jobs. The products that command premium pricing and strong retention often satisfy the emotional and social jobs better than competitors — even when the functional difference is small.

Example: Two project management tools with nearly identical feature sets. Tool A markets itself as "all your tasks in one place." Tool B markets itself as "feel confident your team will hit every deadline." Tool B wins on the emotional job.

**How to discover the JTBD for your product:**

Interview customers who recently purchased (within 30 days) and those who recently churned. Ask:
- "What were you doing before you found us? What was the problem?"
- "When did you first realize you needed something like this?"
- "What finally pushed you to look for a solution?"
- "What else did you consider? Why did you choose us instead?"
- "What do you wish we did better?"

Look for the moment of transition (the "push" and "pull" forces that triggered the search and the decision).

**When to use it:**
- When positioning feels generic or when competitive differentiation is weak.
- When customers say they love the product but cannot clearly explain why to others (the message is not matching the actual job).
- When designing a landing page and deciding what the headline should say.
- Before any major messaging refresh.

**The "milkshake insight" (Christensen's famous example):** McDonald's found that their milkshake's biggest competition was not other milkshakes — it was bananas, granola bars, and bagels. Why? Commuters were hiring the milkshake to do a specific job: provide a satisfying, engaging, slow-consuming activity for a boring morning commute. The job was entertainment and satiety, not refreshment. Improving the milkshake to do that job better (thicker, longer to consume) increased sales more than any other change.

### 1.3 Circle of Competence

**What it is:** Everyone has a domain where they have genuine expertise (their circle of competence) and a much larger domain where they have opinions but not expertise. Good decision-making requires knowing which domain a given decision falls in.

**Marketing application:** Marketing requires opinions about many things (brand strategy, audience psychology, channel selection, copy quality). The circle of competence framework prevents confident-but-wrong decisions.

**Practical use:**
- Before giving advice on a channel you have limited experience with, acknowledge the gap and recommend testing before committing.
- Distinguish between "I believe this will work" (hypothesis) and "I know this will work" (evidence from experience).
- Identify which members of the team or which agencies have genuine depth in each channel. Do not let your seniority substitute for their expertise.
- When evaluating a new marketing channel, honestly assess: have you or someone on your team actually built a competent program in this channel before?

**The failure mode:** Confident ignorance. The marketer who has read about TikTok advertising but has never run a campaign treating their opinion as equivalent to someone with 200 campaigns of experience.

**When to use it:**
- When tempted to implement a strategy primarily because it worked for a different company in a different context.
- When someone argues for a tactic based on their intuition rather than evidence.
- When a consultant or agency presents a recommendation — ask what their direct, recent experience in your specific situation is.

### 1.4 Theory of Constraints

**What it is:** In any system, there is exactly one constraint that limits the overall throughput of the system. Improving any part of the system other than the constraint produces no improvement in overall output. Identifying and fixing the constraint is the highest-leverage action available.

**Origin:** Eliyahu Goldratt, "The Goal."

**Marketing application:** The marketing funnel has a constraint — the one step where the most opportunity is lost. Optimizing upstream of the constraint without fixing it does not improve results. Optimizing downstream of it does not matter yet.

**How to find the constraint:**

Map your funnel with conversion rates at each stage:

```
Ads → Landing page → Trial signup → Activation → Paid conversion → Retention
 ?%        ?%             ?%            ?%              ?%             ?%
```

The stage with the largest drop-off relative to its potential is the constraint. Fix it before anything else.

**Example:**
```
1,000 ad clicks → 350 landing page visits (65% bounce on ad → landing)
350 landing page visits → 90 trial signups (74% drop on landing page)
90 trial signups → 45 activated users (50% never complete onboarding)
45 activated → 20 paid conversions (44% trial-to-paid)
```

The constraint here is trial activation (50% never complete onboarding). Improving ad performance or landing page conversion will send more people into a broken onboarding funnel. Fix activation first.

**When to use it:**
- When marketing investment is increasing but results are not scaling proportionally.
- When deciding where to spend optimization effort across a multi-step funnel.
- When a new campaign launches and produces traffic but no revenue — the constraint is downstream of the traffic.
- Before hiring (more headcount cannot fix a constrained system).

### 1.5 The Flywheel

**What it is:** A self-reinforcing loop where each action compounds on previous actions to generate momentum. The opposite of a one-time campaign: a flywheel gets easier to spin the faster it goes.

**Marketing flywheels (examples):**

Amazon: More customers → More sellers → More selection → Lower prices → More customers.

Content marketing flywheel: Publish valuable content → Earn SEO traffic → Grow email list → Email drives traffic to new content → New content earns more SEO traffic → List grows faster.

Community flywheel: Members join → Members create content and connections → Community becomes more valuable → More members join → Community creates more value.

**How to identify your flywheel:**
- What makes your marketing better as you do more of it?
- What compounds? (SEO, email list, community, brand, customer referrals, reviews)
- What creates the loop? (Each customer brings the next one, each piece of content attracts the next subscriber)

**Marketing application:**
- Identify the compounding mechanism in your marketing and invest disproportionately in it.
- Resist the temptation to run isolated campaigns when a flywheel opportunity exists.
- Measure flywheel metrics (domain authority, list size, community membership, referral rate) alongside revenue metrics.

**When to use it:**
- When doing strategic planning and deciding where to invest long-term.
- When choosing between a campaign (one-time lift) and a program (compounding asset).
- When building a content, SEO, or community strategy.

## Part 2: Expanded Cognitive Biases Reference

15 biases with definitions, marketing application, and worked example.

---

### 2.1 Availability Heuristic

**Definition:** Probability and importance are judged based on how easily examples come to mind. Vivid, recent, emotionally charged events feel more probable.

**Marketing application:** Make positive outcomes easy to imagine with specific case studies, vivid outcome descriptions, and before-and-after visuals.

**Example:**
- Weak: "Our customers see great results."
- Strong: "Marcus reduced his customer support volume by 62% in the first month. Here's how."

**When it works against you:** If your category has recent high-profile failures or your competitors have had scandals, potential customers have vivid negative associations loaded. Name and address them directly.

---

### 2.2 Confirmation Bias

**Definition:** People seek, notice, and remember information that confirms their existing beliefs. They minimize contradictory information.

**Marketing application:** Research what your audience already believes and frame your message to confirm it rather than contradict it.

**Example:**
Your audience believes "most marketing agencies overpromise and underdeliver."
- Weak positioning: "We're different — we really do deliver results."
- Strong positioning: "You're right to be skeptical. Here are 12 clients who'll tell you what actually happened. No agency testimonial page — just their phone numbers."

---

### 2.3 Loss Aversion

**Definition:** Losses feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable. People are more motivated to avoid losing $100 than to gain $100.

**Marketing application:** Frame benefits as losses prevented. Cancellation flows, trial expiration sequences, and FOMO campaigns all leverage loss aversion.

**Example:**
- Gain frame: "Upgrade to Pro and get advanced analytics."
- Loss frame: "Your trial ends in 48 hours. After that, you'll lose access to your saved reports and historical data."

---

### 2.4 Anchoring Bias

**Definition:** The first number seen exerts a disproportionate influence on all subsequent judgments. People adjust from the anchor but not far enough.

**Marketing application:** Control what number is seen first. Show problem cost before product cost. Show highest price tier first.

**Example:**
- No anchor: "Our enterprise plan is $499/month."
- Anchored: "Companies that don't automate this process typically spend $4,000-$8,000/month in manual labor. Our enterprise plan is $499/month."

---

### 2.5 Social Proof (Bandwagon Effect)

**Definition:** Under uncertainty, people look to others to determine correct behavior. The behavior of similar others is especially influential.

**Marketing application:** Use specific, quantified, and credibly similar social proof throughout the funnel.

**Example:**
- Weak: "Thousands of happy customers."
- Strong: "Join 14,200 growth marketers who've already made this their primary lead source. Average time to first result: 11 days."

---

### 2.6 Status Quo Bias

**Definition:** People prefer the current state of affairs. Change requires overcoming the discomfort of uncertainty and the effort of switching.

**Marketing application:** Make switching feel risk-free and make the cost of not switching vivid.

**Example:**
- Feature-focused: "Switch to our platform and get 40+ integrations."
- Status quo override: "Every month you stay on your current setup costs you approximately [calculated number] in manual work. We migrate everything for free. Setup takes one afternoon."

---

### 2.7 Paradox of Choice

**Definition:** Beyond a moderate number of options, more choices lead to paralysis, lower satisfaction, and a higher likelihood of choosing nothing.

**Marketing application:** Limit pricing tiers to three, always highlight a recommended option, reduce the number of CTAs per page to one.

**Example:**
- 7 pricing tiers with no recommendation → choice paralysis, lower conversion
- 3 pricing tiers with "Most Popular" label on middle tier → clear path, higher conversion

---

### 2.8 Peak-End Rule

**Definition:** People judge experiences by their emotional peak (most intense moment) and their ending, not the average.

**Marketing application:** Design the onboarding aha moment and the post-cancellation experience with disproportionate care.

**Example:** A user's 90-day experience with a product is mostly average. But if their first week included a memorable "wow" moment (seeing their data visualized beautifully for the first time) and their cancellation was handled graciously with a personal email, they remember the experience positively.

---

### 2.9 Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Definition:** People continue investing in something because of past investment (time, money, effort), even when continuing is no longer the rational choice.

**Marketing application:** Make users invest in setup, customization, and configuration during onboarding. The more they invest, the higher the psychological cost of switching.

**Example:** Onboarding that requires the user to import their data, connect integrations, invite teammates, and customize their workspace creates sunk cost investment. A simple, frictionless setup creates less psychological lock-in.

**Ethics note:** Designing onboarding to be effortful for the purpose of creating artificial lock-in is a dark pattern. Design it to be effortful because each step delivers genuine value that makes the platform better for the user.

---

### 2.10 Framing Effect

**Definition:** The same information presented in different ways produces different decisions. How a fact is described matters as much as the fact itself.

**Marketing application:** Frame the same offer in terms of what people keep (gain frame) vs. what they avoid losing (loss frame). Test both.

**Example:**
- "95% of surgeries with this technique are successful." (Gain frame)
- "5% of surgeries with this technique result in complications." (Loss frame)
Same data. Loss frame produces more caution. Choose frame based on the desired response.

---

### 2.11 The Mere Exposure Effect (Familiarity Bias)

**Definition:** Repeated exposure to something, without any other information, increases liking and positive evaluation.

**Marketing application:** Consistent brand presence across channels — even without direct response goals — increases liking through familiarity. This is the psychological basis for brand advertising.

**Example:** A prospect sees your ads on LinkedIn five times without clicking. When they later encounter your brand on a review site, they instinctively view it more favorably than a brand they are seeing for the first time.

**Implication:** Reach and frequency in brand campaigns have value that does not show up in direct response metrics.

---

### 2.12 Endowment Effect

**Definition:** People assign higher value to things once they own or feel ownership over them. The psychological value of possessing something is higher than the value of acquiring it.

**Marketing application:** Free trials and "try before you buy" models leverage the endowment effect — once users have experienced the product as theirs, the psychological cost of canceling is higher than the cost of subscribing.

**Example:** "Your workspace, your data, your settings — everything you've set up is ready. Just continue your subscription to keep it." (Trial-to-paid conversion email)

---

### 2.13 Reciprocity Norm

**Definition:** Receiving something creates a strong social obligation to give something back in return, disproportionate to the value of the original gift.

**Marketing application:** Give genuinely before asking. The perceived generosity of the gift determines the strength of the reciprocal obligation.

**Example:** A marketing tool that publishes a genuinely useful free report, no email required, with no mention of their product within it, generates more trust and downstream demand than a gated lead magnet with a hard product pitch inside.

---

### 2.14 Scarcity Bias

**Definition:** Perceived scarcity increases perceived value. Things that are hard to get are assumed to be more valuable and more desirable.

**Marketing application:** Limited cohort sizes, waitlists, time-limited pricing, and early-access programs all leverage scarcity. Must be real.

**Example:** "We're accepting 20 new clients in Q2. Applications are open until April 30." (Genuine scarcity in a service business — real capacity constraints.)

---

### 2.15 Dunning-Kruger Effect

**Definition:** People with limited knowledge in a domain tend to overestimate their competence, while people with deep expertise tend to underestimate it.

**Marketing application:** Your audience may overestimate their ability to solve the problem your product addresses. Respectfully demonstrate the complexity — not to embarrass them, but to establish the need for your solution.

**Example:** A DIY SEO audience may believe their current approach is solid. A content piece titled "We audited 500 'healthy' websites. Here's what we actually found" uses the Dunning-Kruger effect: the prospect believes they're the exception, but curiosity drives them to check.

## Part 3: Copy Rewrite Examples

Five before-and-after rewrites showing the psychological principles at work. Each includes the principle applied and why the "after" version is more effective.

---

### Rewrite 1: Landing Page Headline

**Context:** B2B SaaS — project management tool for creative agencies.

**Before (no psychology):**
> "The Project Management Tool Built for Agencies"

**After (psychology applied):**
> "Stop explaining to clients why projects are late. Finally."

**Principles used:**
- **Confirmation bias:** Creative agency project managers know this pain. They expect this headline to be about them.
- **Loss aversion:** Framed around the painful thing they want to avoid (client conversations about delays).
- **Availability heuristic:** "Finally" evokes the vivid feeling of having looked for this solution for a long time.
- **Pattern interrupt:** The period after "Finally" is unexpected and creates a pause that implies the search is over.

**Why it works:** The original headline describes what the product is. The rewrite describes what the customer escapes. The customer does not want a project management tool — they want to stop having uncomfortable conversations with clients.

---

### Rewrite 2: Pricing Page

**Context:** Email marketing platform, three pricing tiers.

**Before (no psychology):**
> Starter: $29/month. Pro: $79/month. Business: $149/month.

**After (psychology applied):**

```
Business: $149/month
Everything in Pro, plus dedicated onboarding, priority support, and custom reporting.

Pro: $79/month    ← MOST POPULAR
Unlimited subscribers, all automations, A/B testing, and everything most growing
teams need. Used by 78% of our customers.

Starter: $29/month
Up to 5,000 subscribers. Perfect for getting started.
```

**Principles used:**
- **Anchoring:** Business tier ($149) displayed first, anchoring the reader's sense of what the product costs.
- **Decoy effect:** Business tier makes Pro look like value; Starter tier gives price-sensitive entry point while making Pro feel reachable.
- **Social proof:** "Used by 78% of our customers" on the middle tier applies social proof at the exact moment of pricing decision.
- **Paradox of choice:** Three tiers, not six. Clear "most popular" signal removes the burden of reasoning from scratch.
- **Authority:** Specific percentage (78%) is more credible than "most popular" alone.

---

### Rewrite 3: Email Subject Line (Trial Expiration)

**Context:** SaaS productivity tool, trial ending in 48 hours.

**Before (no psychology):**
> "Your free trial is ending soon — upgrade today"

**After (psychology applied):**
> "Your shortcuts and templates disappear in 48 hours"

**Principles used:**
- **Loss aversion:** Names specific things that will be lost, not abstract "access."
- **Availability heuristic:** "Shortcuts and templates" are vivid, specific — the reader can immediately picture what they will lose.
- **Endowment effect:** The reader has created these things. They feel like possessions.
- **Peak-end rule:** The email targets the sunk cost of the trial work invested.

**Why it works:** The original tells the prospect what to do (upgrade). The rewrite tells the prospect what they will lose if they do not. The loss of something already created and owned is far more motivating than the gain of a continued subscription.

---

### Rewrite 4: Ad Copy (Facebook/LinkedIn)

**Context:** B2B lead generation tool targeting sales teams.

**Before (no psychology):**
> "Generate more leads with our powerful prospecting platform. Try it free."

**After (psychology applied):**

> "Your SDRs are spending 60% of their time on research that doesn't convert.
>
> We analyzed 10,000 hours of sales rep activity. The pattern: manual prospecting eats the hours that should go to selling.
>
> [Tool] gives reps verified contacts, intent signals, and personalized outreach — in the time it currently takes to build a prospect list.
>
> 2,200 B2B sales teams made the switch last year.
>
> See what you're leaving on the table →"

**Principles used:**
- **Availability heuristic:** Specific number (60%, 10,000 hours) makes the problem vivid and real.
- **Confirmation bias:** Sales leaders already believe their reps waste time on non-selling activities. This confirms it.
- **Social proof:** "2,200 B2B sales teams" quantifies the bandwagon.
- **Loss aversion:** "What you're leaving on the table" frames the CTA as recovering a loss, not gaining a benefit.
- **Authority:** "We analyzed 10,000 hours of sales rep activity" — original research signals credibility.

**Why it works:** The original is about the product's capabilities. The rewrite is about the prospect's current problem, validated with data, confirmed by peers, and framed as a loss to be recovered.

---

### Rewrite 5: Onboarding Email (Day 3 — Low Activation)

**Context:** SaaS platform. User signed up but has not connected their data source.

**Before (no psychology):**
> "Subject: Get started with [Product] today!
>
> Hi [Name], don't forget to connect your data source to start seeing insights. Click here to get started."

**After (psychology applied):**

> **Subject:** Your dashboard is empty — here's why that matters
>
> Hey [Name],
>
> You signed up 3 days ago, but your dashboard is still waiting.
>
> Right now, you're flying blind on the metrics that actually matter. Every day without connected data is another day of decisions made on gut feeling instead of numbers.
>
> Teams that connect their first data source within the first week are 3x more likely to still be active at 90 days.
>
> It takes 4 minutes. Here's the exact step:
>
> [Connect your first data source →]
>
> After that, I'll show you the three reports our most successful customers set up first.
>
> — [Name], Customer Success at [Product]

**Principles used:**
- **Loss aversion:** "Flying blind" frames inaction as ongoing loss, not just missed gain.
- **Anchoring:** "3x more likely to still be active at 90 days" — anchors the reader to what consistent users do, creating social norm pressure.
- **BJ Fogg (ability):** "It takes 4 minutes" reduces perceived effort. "Here's the exact step" further reduces the friction of starting.
- **Commitment-consistency:** Signed up = prior commitment. "Your dashboard is waiting" implies consistency with that commitment means taking the next step.
- **Reciprocity:** Offering to show "the three reports our most successful customers set up first" after the step — a gift promised on completion.
- **Peak-end rule:** The email is designed to create a positive moment when they connect and see data appear for the first time.
